ALTwitter

#hakunametadata
Twitter metadata profiles of the Members of European Parliament

Summary

One of the main reasons why metadata is used extensively for surveillance and advertisements is because of its extensive capability to capture more dimensions of the useful information than the data itself. Interestingly, the ordinary internet users fail to see the mysterious nature of metadata because it is invisible to naked eyes. Law enforcement agencies and the advertisers make the best use of the invisibility of metadata to track, profile and target us. For them, the value of metadata is more than the data itself for the following reasons:

  • Size vs. efficiency of processing: Data is too big to handle, whereas metadata is not even one tenth of its size (in most cases); but, in spite of its size, metadata gives the gist of the data.
  • Protection by encryption: Data can be easily encrypted, whereas metadata cannot be protected easily by encryption. Even services (such as Signal) designed specifically to protect the privacy of its content fails to completely protect metadata of communication in many adversarial scenarios.
  • Ease of combining with other sources: Data is rigid when it comes to combining with data from other sources to extract useful insights together. On the other hand, doing the same with metadata is relatively easy for the reason that it is always loosely structured and it gives different insight with different data sources.
  • Loosely bound privacy regulations: Like it is said before, a combination of metadata from various sources, gives out new insights which are difficult to predict beforehand. Because of this uncertainty, many privacy regulations cannot be narrowed down to restrict certain kinds of metadata. Even the least obvious or most required metadata (for the purpose of the normal functioning of an Internet service, for security troubleshooting or record-keeping) can be explored for understanding unambiguously sensitive insights.

ALTwitter was initially intended to build a Twitter-like profiles of the members of European Parliament (MEPs) from their Twitter metadata. However, after its initial release, we thought that the insights aggregated from the metadata of all the MEPs can be as interesting as their individual metadata profiles. Also, it can be used to explain how the advertisement industry benefits from the unregulated metadata and where the everyday internet users become the product for targeted ads.

#1 Size doesn’t matter

Metadata hasn’t spared anyone! Especially when it comes to Twitter metadata, whether it is FBI's Director James Comey or an online bully who once had a Utopian dream of staying anonymous, have been the victims of not noticing the invisible power of metadata. Like it is mentioned before, the size of metadata is small compared to that of the data from which it originates.

But, does this small size impact the insights that we can infer?

No, the size doesn’t matter at all.

Even with a small population and a study conducted over a short time window, the researchers from Standford were able to deduce unambiguously sensitive information such as medical conditions and firearm ownership completely based on phone metadata (Call Data Records).

Figure 1: Number of tweets used for the ALTwitter project from the MEPs according to their nationality

In this realm, even though the dataset used for ALTwitter project just contains 617 participants (MEPs) and metadata from a total approximate of ~10000 tweets is sufficient enough to show the power of metadata. Based on the number of tweets considered for the project, the treemap visualization depicts the share of EU nations in our dataset. Not so surprisingly, the countries with more members to the parliament (such as Germany, France, and Italy) have tweeted the most. If we take individual contributions and not as country-wise cumulative aggregation, we might notice that the MEPs from Italy tweets the most.

#2 Target based on how people access Twitter (Mobile vs Web)

We have the limitation of just 140 characters for tweeting. That isn't too much, right? If we consider the content of tweet as data, rest everything is the metadata - which includes time of the tweet, sources used for tweeting, geolocation tags, and much more as shown in the figure below. These small chunks are actually bigger than the data that we post on Twitter; however, this goes unnoticed.

We thought of concentrating on one set of metadata for the sake of simpler explanation, and it happens to the sources from which tweets are originated. Grouping the users into "web" and "mobile" is one of the primary steps of targeted ads because the advertisement strategy for mobile users and web users differ completely. Also, Twitter business suggests to ensure that the Ads are relevant and optimized for a mobile audience as 80% of the tweets originates from mobile devices. Interestingly, it was found that "3 out of 5" mobile users from the UK are on Twitter while watching TV (source), based on the fact that Twitter allows the advertisers to include gender, device, language or geo-targeting to TV targeted campaigns.

Contradicting to these results, the MEPs turned out to be using the Web version of the Twitter compared to that of mobile apps or other mobile based services. Also, there is an affinity towards Apple products over all other devices.

#3 Target based on device/service used (for credit scoring)

Continuing from the previous point of targeting ads specific to mobile devices, all the MEPs use mobile devices for tweeting. It is possible to find the financial status of the users based on what mobile devices they use. The simple fact that "Apple devices are expensive than Android devices" could tell that MEPs using Apple devices are financially more stable than those who use other devices. We plotted the graph for all the countries in the EU based on their device usage to see if countries with lower income show a peak in non-Apple device usage. Some countries such as Bulgaria, Latvia and Slovakia show this pattern. Nonetheless, depicting anything beyond this is not accurate, as most of the MEPs are well paid. If at all the same analogy can be applied to a specific region, a specific neighborhood of a city, then it is indeed easy to find which part of the city contains rich people and which are some of the poor neighborhoods. An example of such details on a global scale (~3 Billion tweets) can be found here (reference.

Adtech industry is beyond this! They rely on the consumer analysis [1], especially by conducting sentiment analysis to predict the financial market for a potential ad that is customized for a user such that the chances that he clicks that ad is high. Along with the metadata such as time, gender, geolocation, device, hashtags, etc., these industries also rely on the text corpora of the Tweets by a user. These indicators will be further used for credit scoring - e.g. determining the insurance premiums.

#4 Target based on categories of devices/services used for tweeting

Social media has gone beyond being a platform to stay in touch with friends, and Twitter is no exception. People use Twitter as an advertisement forum - to divert traffic to their websites, and as campaigning tool - to reach out to bigger proportion of the audience. In this realm, the source from which a tweet is originated can be classified into following groups:

  • Source Type: Such as web widget, mobile app, desktop app, etc.
  • Source Categories based on their purpose: Twitter client, Engagement/ Marketing, Entertainment, etc.
  • Source Origin: Mostly based on the manufacturer of the device or service.

We found 121 different tweet sources, which we classified into 7 different types, 6 categories , and 16 origins. A visualization of the same can be found in the figure below. If you are keen on finding more information on this classification, this visualization of the same in different format gives better readability.

Twitter allows the advertisers to target the users based on their installed app category. That is -- if I have used downloaded an app for campaigning - say Voicestrom, advertisers can get the information that the user have installed an app from the category "Third-party engagement". #4 Target based on uniqueness of the users (fingerprinting)

Metadata from large databases are often stored in the form of a specific structure so that the processing of metadata done in an efficient manner. For example, in our case, we had listed the whole list of sources (device/service) used by the MEPs as the column names and the MEPs' names as a row. These kind of structural metadata is sparse in nature - i.e. MEP "A" uses device/service 'X', 'Y', 'Z', MEP "B" uses 'X', 'XX', 'Y'. The sparse datasets suffer from robust de-anonymization attacks: more the number of sources/devices, more unique will be the pattern "X-Y-Z" or "X-XX-Y". So, fingerprinting the uniqueness and matching it with other data to de-anonymize them is much easier for the advertisers. The tail of the graph of all the device/services vs the number of tweets contains a lot of entries with value 1. So, by comparing that with the figure below, we can informally identify the MEPs uniquely just based on their metadata.

Figure 6: Third-party entertainment apps used by the MEPs

Most of the apps allow the users to share something on Twitter via their in-app sharing features. Though we haven't checked manually, most of these third-party entertainment apps do the same. Otherwise, there is no reason why would someone from the parliament use Temple Run or Banana Kon from their official phone? Well, we don't have an answer for that. Possibly, they were super excited and tweeted their score ;)

There are plenty of examples, but some of the interesting uniqueness that we identified are as follows. This uniqueness can be used to profile you and deliver custom-made ads.

  • There is just one MEP (Roberts Zile) who uses the Vine App, a camera app for making 6 second looping videos on their Android device.
  • There is just one MEP (Alberto Cirio) who uses a BlackBerry Phone, just one (Davor Skrlec) who owns a Sony Experia phone, and just one (Renato Soru) who uses a Samsung Tablet.
  • It looks like the Romanian MEP Emilian Pavel is into sports because he is the only who uses a Sports tracker app.
  • Last but not least, not so surprisingly mobile gaming apps are not so famous among the MEPs except for Teresa J-Becerril who is into Temple Run and Angelika Niebler who's metadata says she uses Banana Kong.

We found its most likely that all the MEPs have one password for their individual official Twitter accounts, and they share it with their team. They do not use the multi-user login feature of Twitter, but they share their passwords. So, it is possible that one of the interns used Temple Run/ Banana Kong, and not the MEP themself.

#5 Target based on schedule/calendar

Last but not least the advertisers can monetize one's schedule/calendar by inferring it from metadata. As seen on the individual metadata-based profiles of the MEPs, one can learn "who is active, and at what time?" from their Twitter metadata. So, the advertisers have to target the users when they show more activities to increase the chances of clicking a promotional ad on Twitter.
